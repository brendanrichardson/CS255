### **CS-255 System Analysis and Design**

**Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?**

DriverPass was the client for this project. They wanted a web based system that helps students prepare for DMV driving tests by combining online practice exams with driving lesson scheduling. The system needed to support multiple users (customers, office staff, instructors, management, and IT) and allow customers to track test progress while also letting staff manage lesson reservations and reporting.

**What did you do particularly well?**

I did well at translating the interview transcript into clear requirements and then carrying those requirements through to the design. I also think my UML diagrams were a strong part of the project because they helped show the system from different viewpoints. Keeping the design consistent across the requirements document, diagrams, and technical requirements was something I focused on.

**If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?**

If I could revise one part, I would expand the technical requirements and limitations sections to be more detailed and realistic. For example, I would include more specifics about how data backups and recovery would be handled, how scalability would be approached during peak usage, and what metrics would define “acceptable performance.” I would also tighten a few assumptions so they are more clearly tied to what the client provided versus what I inferred.

**How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?**

I interpreted the user’s needs by identifying the core goals DriverPass described (improving student pass rates, making lesson scheduling easier, and tracking progress) then converting those ideas into measurable functional and nonfunctional requirements. Those requirements directly guided my UML diagrams and class design. Considering user needs is important because the system only succeeds if it fits how people actually work. If a design ignores real workflows, it can create confusion, errors, and extra support needs even if the software is technically correct.

**How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?**

My approach is to start with requirements and user workflows, then design the system in layers. I begin with a high-level view of who uses the system and what they need to do. Next I map key workflows step by step to confirm the logic is correct. Then I define the data structure needed to support those workflows. In future projects, I will continue using a blended approach that combines process modeling and object modeling, and I will also spend more time validating requirements early by checking for edge cases, constraints, and roles and permissions before moving into detailed design.
